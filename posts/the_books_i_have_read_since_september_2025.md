# The Books I Have Read Since September 2025

It has been a *very* long time since I have written an update on the literature I have been consuming. In the interest of keeping a written record, I'd like to provide a brief one now.

As I write this, I am sitting in a coffee shop in Lakewood, Colorado participating in an event called *Shut Up & Write!* I came here with a friend, [Jason Adams](https://jason.blog/), to get a better feel for novelist culture. 

As we walked in, I immediately felt like I stuck out like a sore thumb. Most of the people here are novelists or graphic novelists. These are serious, for real, literal, __writers__. While most here are writing fiction and making worlds, I sit here writing in my little blog. I am in awe of their creativity and spirit.

While they sit down to write deep and lengthy fiction, I'm writing a recap of the fiction and nonfiction I've read. Without further ado, let's dive in.

## Butterick's Practical Typography

This is the only book on today's list that is virtually free to anyone with an internet connection. In fact, it might be the only book I've __ever__ mentioned on this blog that is free in that way. You can read it on [its official website](https://practicaltypography.com/).

Matthew Butterick is a lawyer who, at my best estimate, loves typography.
I assume his interest grew from a distaste for the amount of poorly formatted paperwork floating around the lawyer world, but I can't know for sure.

Before reading Butterick's guide, I never considered how critical typography is in the perception of the written word.

I won't go over __all__ the various typographical rules Butterick covers — not least because you can go read them for yourself — but I do feel a desire to highlight a few that I found stimulating.

### When in Doubt, Use Charter

[Charter](https://practicaltypography.com/charter.html) is a typeface designed for easy readability on a wide variety of digital devices.
Its angular letterforms were originally laid out to fit within the limited memory of older computer systems. Today, they're generally well optimized for the limited bandwidth that often afflicts websites. 

In the early days of this blog, I decided, somewhat arbitrarily, to use Charter. 
As I read Butterick's guide, I realized that I had somehow struck gold without realizing it.

I've grown to love Charter. It's beautiful.

### Small Caps Cannot Be Had for Free

Small caps lettering (and a great many other kinds of formatting) require a lot of effort to get right. They are almost never typeset well automatically and usually require some manual intervention.

For those caught out of the know, [small caps](https://practicaltypography.com/small-caps.html) are letterforms that are the shape of "normal" capital letters, but formatted the size of lowercase letters.

As Butterick says, it's almost impossible to find a free font (including Charter) with good small caps built-in. I highlight this particular rule because it demonstrates a pattern that pervades all of typography: It's hard to get **everything** exactly right. 

It's more of an art than a science.

### You __Should__ Read a Book on Typography

You should read a book on Typography.
Any book.
It does not need to be this one.

If you write anything of substance with any kind of regularity — which, if you're reading this, is extremely likely — it's important that you understand the hidden rules behind how readers expect letters to be formatted. 

Butterick's guide especially is an explanation for why an attention to detail is important. To then writer, none of these things seem important. To the reader, though, it can make a world of a difference.
