# How to Place Humanity in a Technological World

The book _Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance: An Inquiry into Values_ can be viewed in two distinct ways. 
From one perspective, it is a literal manual on how to more effectively solve problems and maintain motorcycles.
From another, it's a reflection on human progress itself.

## Summary

The narrative of _Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance_ centers on the thoughts of a father during a cross-country cycling trip.
He begins by musing the nature of his companions, the Sutherlands, relationship to technology.
Certain observations, such as the refusal to maintain their own motorcycle, or fix a dripping facet, led the protagonist to believe they have a certain fear of technology.
Initially, he believed this fear was founded on the idea that science, technology, math and finance are inherently inhuman.
Rather, that these ``square'' fields of study's attempted objectivity were dangerous and unnatural.
The narrator noted that this fear was in stark contrast to his companion's reliance on science and technology in their daily lives.

He labeled this mindset "romantic" and moved onto to describe its opposite: "classical".
Classic minds try to understand science and technology, without ascribing any emotional judgment to them.
While his companion John Sutherland, a romantic, chooses to hire mechanics to fix his bike, the narrator maintains his own.

As the cycling trip continues, it is revealed that at some point in the narrator's past, he had undergone electroconvulsive therapy.
The result: his previous personality, named ``Phaedrus'', was erased.
The remainder of the cycling trip is focused on the narrator's relationship with–and memory of–this previous personality's descent into insanity. 

Phaedrus was a professor of rhetoric at a small college in Montana.
The narrator remembers a ``seed crystal'' \parencite*[180]{zen}, a moment that lead to the realization the book ultimately centers on.
It was a moment of passing. A coworker said to him, ``I hope you are teaching Quality to your students.''
No epiphany occurred, only confusion. 
The question of ``what is Quality'' held Phaedrus in an iron-like vice.

He knew of the existence of aesthetics, a study entirely focused on defining Quality, but believed that the field tended to define it as a tool.
Phaedrus despised defining Quality in any way that ``subordinated'' it \parencite*[180]{zen}.
He decided to assert that Quality is undefinable.

His journey though the metaphysical space of Quality continued in his teaching.
He devised a kind of experiment.
He asked his students to estimate the quality of their own work before submission.
Additionally, he postponed the announcement of all grades until after the semester was over.

The theory was that the existence of a grading system distracted students from the essential goal of their education: learning.
Phaedrus predicted that students would immediately, and temporarily become lazy and the quality of their work would decline.
Then, realizing their mistake, they would scramble, turn in outstanding work out of fear of failing the class.
He suspected that this scramble would acclimate the students to defining their own measure of Quality work,
a mindset that is both more productive (since the strict definition of success found in some classrooms is less accurate to real-world work)
and significantly more enjoyable.

The experiment confirmed his hypothesis. 
His students universally performed better without a grading system.
Further, Phaedrus surveyed the students. Those who were already high performers preferred the new system, while the low performers wanted to revert.
While this proved nothing definitively,
Phaedrus believed this signaled the top performers were already motivated by the satisfaction of creating quality work,
and the lowest performers motivated by a passing grade.

Unfortunately, he could not continue his experiment after the semester ended. 
In order to continue teaching his students to guide their writing by Quality, Phaedrus needed to define Quality to his superiors.
Since he believed Quality 'undefinable', he had no recourse.

The narrator of _Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance_ frequently references concepts as hierarchies.
Since he had solved the question of "what _is_ Quality," he moved to "where is Quality."

One might place Quality within the object of one's focus.
For example, a painting has inherent quality.

Moreover, the nature of the romantic mode it's contrast with the classical mode becomes clearer with this definition of Quality.
Indeed, it becomes a spectrum.
Romantic interactions place the subject and the observer further apart.
The observer values the inherent nature of the object, and chooses less analysis, since that would thereby remove the object from its inherent nature.
Classical interactions, however, occur when the observer values the evolution of both observer and subject through analysis.

This entire description takes place in the mind of the narrator as he rides down the highway, child in tow.
They stop in the town Phaedrus' college resides and meet his friends from before the ECS treatment.
The discussion of Quality culminates in a hike up a steep mountain. 

The subject of much of the remaining journey is one's motivation.
While the discussion of Quality remained comparatively abstract, this new topic demonstrably pertains to motorcycle maintenance.
The narrator describes how to avoid and recover from two distinct kinds of "gumption traps" \parencite*[304]{zen}.

The first gumption trap: setbacks.
These are external obstacles to getting the job done.
For example, forgetting a critical step in a process, and only realizing after completion the mistake.
Then it becomes necessary to reverse all the work and do it all over again.
The narrator claims the main way to solve or avoid this issue is through careful planning and execution.
In other words, simply don't make the mistake in the first place.
Most setback-type gumption traps relate to waiting.
It can sometimes be better to do something without professional help, simply to avoid waiting and losing gumption.

The second gumption trap: hang-ups.   
These are deep internal issues that sap motivation and tend to be a bit more intense than setbacks.
Occasionally, a situation arises where a mechanic hits a roadblock.
He doesn't know what to do, and it is not from a lack of ideas. 
There are so many possible tests to run, fixes to try, he is overwhelmed.
The narrator claims this is a problem of values.
The mechanic's individual ability to sort out potential next steps, is confused.
Quality, has become fuzzy for him.
The best solution to a problem such as this, is to step back and reexamine his values.
By spending time to define his definition of success, the mechanic can narrow now his next steps to a small set.

Hang-up gumption traps share a common theme: they occur when the mechanics judgement of Quality is impaired.
Possibly the most common kind of hang-up is boredom.
Over time, repetitive projects can become less and less interesting.
According to the narrator, the best solution to this issue is to simply stop working on the project.
Continued work on a boring section of the project builds resentment and frustration.
It also makes it more difficult to look at the problem analytically. 
By taking a break, the mind returns with a fresh perspective, possibly finding an alternate solution that is more engaging.
Boredom arrives when the mechanic has acclimated to the project, devaluing it.
By leaving and returning, he resets his judgment closer to where he was when the project began.

The book concludes by describing the final stages of Phaedrus' descent into insanity.
After he determined his work on Quality would be neither appreciated nor nurtured in his previous place of work, 
he applied for a position at the University of Chicago, attracted by its Committee on Analysis of Ideas and Study of Methods.
From a distance, it appeared to be collection of people who had discovered similar insights.

During the application process, and after his acceptance, it became clear this was not the case.
Thus follows an extended battle between the Phaedrus, a student and professor of rhetoric and the Chairman of the committee,
a student of Greek philosophy and dialectic.
While victorious, it becomes clear to Phaedrus, and to those in his vicinity, that his philosophy has brought him far outside
the realm of conventional human thought.
In other words, insane.
His self-care plummets, and he is eventually taken to the hospital, where he undergoes electroconvulsive therapy to erase his personality.

The story concludes with a reconciliation between the narrator and his past self, Phaedrus.
As the narrator has recovered a significant portion of Phaedrus' memories, he now sees the two as one.

## A Reflection of Human Progress in Technology

By defining the relationship between the romantic and classical modes of expression,
the narrator has incidentally demonstrated a key point in the advancement of human technology.

Much technology developed today exists to reduce the amount of time required to perform otherwise tedious, boring, and mentally disengaging work.
Examples include arithmetic to photo developing to data entry.
While all these jobs are important, their use is rarely to directly solve a problem, rather, they often exist as a component of larger issues.
It is also true that they are rapidly being replaced by computerized alternatives. 
The key differentiator between jobs in danger of computerization or robotization is whether they require a judgement of quality.

Professions normally associated with romantic modes of thought, like visual artist, musician, or novelist are safe,
since art definitionally conveys a part of the human experience. 
Thus, statistical aggregations like Midjourney or ChatGPT cannot fulfil the role.

Professions that are conventionally associated with classical modes of thought, like researchers, engineers, or businessmen are also safe.
These professions exist to solve new human problems, a task that cannot be fulfilled by a tool designed to solve old human problems.

Unfortunately, for topics like mathematics, the question becomes: how to teach analytical reasoning and _human_ problem-solving
without falling back to work that computers are already quite good at, and that require no Quality judgement (like computing the derivative of a function).
This is an intimidating question.
For now, _Zen and Art of Motorcycle Maintenance_ suggests that the answer revolves around building good values, both morally, and of Quality.
